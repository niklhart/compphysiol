Tutorial: Defining a sampling scheme

To run a simulation in the Computational Physiology Toolbox, the individual components of a simulation task must be specified. In this tutorial, we will look at how to specify a sampling scheme.

Defining a SamplingScheme or SamplingRange object via function Sampling

Function Sampling allows to specify at which timepoints and for which model observables a model prediction should be computed. Let us start by defining a simple observable (the class Observable is explained in another tutorial):
obs1 = Observable('SimplePK','pla','total','Mass/Volume')
obs1 =
Observable object: SimplePK|Site:pla,Binding:total,UnitType:Mass/Volume
Now, you can use function Sampling() to specify a sampling range or sampling timepoints.
If you provide exactly two timepoints, an object of class SamplingRange is created, for which appropriate sampling times within this timeframe will be determined internally by the MATLAB ODE solver:
Sampling([0 24]*u.h, obs1)
ans =
SamplingRange: 0 h - 24 h with Observable object: SimplePK|Site:pla,Binding:total,UnitType:Mass/Volume
When more than two input times are provided, this corresponds to exact sampling times and will result in a SamplingSchedule object. That is, model predictions will be computed at exactly these input times (we store it in a variable s1 for later use):
s1 = Sampling([0 5 24]*u.h, obs1)
s1 =
SamplingSchedule object: Time Observable ---- ------------------------------ 1 0 h SimplePK|pla-total-Mass/Volume 2 5 h SimplePK|pla-total-Mass/Volume 3 24 h SimplePK|pla-total-Mass/Volume
A sampling window generally allows for a more efficient computation in MATLAB, but sampling timepoints can be useful in many circumstances, for example if you want to compare the output of two different models at the same sampling times.
Of note, to create a sampling schedule containing less than three timepoints, use function SamplingSchedule directly instead of Sampling.
We can also create a schedule with several observables:
obs2 = Observable('PBPK',{'adi','mus'},'tis','total','Mass/Volume');
s2 = Sampling([2 7 10]*u.h, obs2)
s2 =
SamplingSchedule object: Time Observable ---- ------------------------------ 1 2 h PBPK|adi-tis-total-Mass/Volume 2 2 h PBPK|mus-tis-total-Mass/Volume 3 7 h PBPK|adi-tis-total-Mass/Volume 4 7 h PBPK|mus-tis-total-Mass/Volume 5 10 h PBPK|adi-tis-total-Mass/Volume 6 10 h PBPK|mus-tis-total-Mass/Volume
and get a schedule for adipose and muscle tissue concentrations.

Combining sampling schedules

Next, SamplingSchedule (but not SamplingRange) objects can also be concatenated:
[s1 s2]
ans =
SamplingSchedule object: Time Observable ---- ------------------------------ 1 0 h SimplePK|pla-total-Mass/Volume 2 5 h SimplePK|pla-total-Mass/Volume 3 24 h SimplePK|pla-total-Mass/Volume 4 2 h PBPK|adi-tis-total-Mass/Volume 5 2 h PBPK|mus-tis-total-Mass/Volume 6 7 h PBPK|adi-tis-total-Mass/Volume 7 7 h PBPK|mus-tis-total-Mass/Volume 8 10 h PBPK|adi-tis-total-Mass/Volume 9 10 h PBPK|mus-tis-total-Mass/Volume
and we get a schedule for plasma concentration and adipose and muscle tissue concentrations at different timepoints.

Extracting sampling schedules from Record objects

In many cases, however, it is not necessary to create Sampling schedules manually. Instead, they can be extracted from experimental measurements.
In the Computational Physiology Toolbox, experimental measurements are stored in Record objects. Function schedule() creates a sampling schedule corresponding to a Record object.
For example, let us consider the following Warfarin plasma data.
data = ImportableData('data/Warfarin_Holford1986.csv','Delimiter',',');
data.maprow('Warfarin plasma concentration','Record','Site','pla');
expid = import(data)
1 experimental dataset(s) imported successfully.
expid =
Individual object: type: 'Experimental data' name: 'ID 1' dosing: '0 dosing events' physiology: '0 parameters defined' observation: '8 records' Use builtin('disp',OBJ)) to see the underlying structure of the Individual object OBJ
The import via ImportableData/import() is explained in another tutorial. Here, we will just focus on the resulting Record object:
expid.observation
ans =
Record object: Time Observable Value ----- ----------------------- -------------- 1 0.5 h ExpData|pla-Mass/Volume 472.4137 ug/L 2 1 h ExpData|pla-Mass/Volume 1079.3103 ug/L 3 2 h ExpData|pla-Mass/Volume 1351.7241 ug/L 4 4 h ExpData|pla-Mass/Volume 1258.6206 ug/L 5 8 h ExpData|pla-Mass/Volume 1110.3448 ug/L 6 12 h ExpData|pla-Mass/Volume 979.3103 ug/L 7 24 h ExpData|pla-Mass/Volume 713.7931 ug/L 8 32 h ExpData|pla-Mass/Volume 641.3793 ug/L
The sampling schedule underlying these measurements can now be easily extracted using function schedule():
schedule(expid.observation)
ans =
SamplingSchedule object: Time Observable ----- ----------------------- 1 0.5 h ExpData|pla-Mass/Volume 2 1 h ExpData|pla-Mass/Volume 3 2 h ExpData|pla-Mass/Volume 4 4 h ExpData|pla-Mass/Volume 5 8 h ExpData|pla-Mass/Volume 6 12 h ExpData|pla-Mass/Volume 7 24 h ExpData|pla-Mass/Volume 8 32 h ExpData|pla-Mass/Volume
This way of retrieving sampling schedules is useful for creating for goodness-of-fit plots or parameter estimation tasks, where you need to compute model predictions corresponding to individual experimental datapoints.
